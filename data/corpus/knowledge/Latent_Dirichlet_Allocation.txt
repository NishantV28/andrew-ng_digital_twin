Source: Journal of Machine Learning Research (2003)
Title: Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA)
Authors: David M. Blei, Andrew Y. Ng, Michael I. Jordan

Content:
Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) is a generative probabilistic model for collections of discrete data such as text corpora. It is a three-level hierarchical Bayesian model, in which each item of a collection is modeled as a finite mixture over an underlying set of topics. Each topic is, in turn, modeled as an infinite mixture over an underlying set of topic probabilities.

In the context of text modeling, the LDA model assumes the following generative process for a document $w$ in a corpus $D$:
1. Choose $N \sim \text{Poisson}(\xi)$ where $N$ is the number of words in the document.
2. Choose $\theta \sim \text{Dirichlet}(\alpha)$ where $\theta$ is the topic distribution for the document.
3. For each of the $N$ words $w_n$:
   a. Choose a topic $z_n \sim \text{Multinomial}(\theta)$.
   b. Choose a word $w_n$ from $p(w_n | z_n, \beta)$, a multinomial probability conditioned on the topic $z_n$.

Key parameters:
- $\alpha$: Parameter of the Dirichlet prior on the per-document topic distributions. A low value of $\alpha$ suggests that documents are represented by only a few topics.
- $\beta$: Parameter of the Dirichlet prior on the per-topic word distributions.
- $\theta$: Document-topic distribution.
- $z$: Vector of topic assignments for each word in the document.
- $w$: Vector of observed words.

LDA generalizes older models like Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) and Probabilistic Latent Semantic Indexing (pLSI). pLSI had a significant drawback: it had no generative model at the level of documents, meaning it could not easily assign probabilities to unseen documents and the number of parameters grew linearly with the size of the training corpus, leading to overfitting. LDA resolves this by treating the topic mixture weights as a k-dimensional Dirichlet random variable.

For inference, Blei, Ng, and Jordan presented a variational EM (Expectation-Maximization) algorithm. Gibbs sampling was later popularized as an alternative inference method.
